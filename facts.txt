Shark species have existed for over 400 million years, even before the dinosaurs.
Great white sharks can swim at speeds of up to 25 miles per hour (40 km/h).
Hammerhead sharks have a wide-set head that enhances their vision and helps detect prey.
The whale shark is the largest species of shark and can grow up to 40 feet (12 meters) long.
The shortfin mako shark is the fastest, reaching speeds of up to 60 miles per hour (96 km/h).
Bull sharks can live in both saltwater and freshwater, even swimming up rivers like the Mississippi.
Tiger sharks eat almost anything, from fish to sea turtles, and even license plates!
Blue sharks are deep-water sharks that can dive up to 1,000 feet (300 meters).
The great hammerhead is the largest species of hammerhead shark, with a head up to 6.5 feet (2 meters) wide.
Lemon sharks are named for their yellow-brown coloration, which helps them blend into sandy seafloors.
Greenland sharks are one of the longest-living vertebrates, with lifespans exceeding 400 years.
Nurse sharks are docile and often rest on the ocean floor during the day.
Sand tiger sharks have sharp teeth that protrude even when their mouths are closed.
Angel sharks are flat, bottom-dwelling sharks that resemble rays and ambush their prey.
Basking sharks are filter feeders and use their huge mouths to scoop up plankton.
Oceanic whitetip sharks are widespread and have distinctive white-tipped fins.
The porbeagle shark is a close relative of the great white and found in cold waters.
Spiny dogfish sharks have sharp spines in front of their dorsal fins for defense.
Silky sharks are sleek and graceful, often found in tropical and subtropical waters.
While not a shark, manta rays are closely related to sharks and share cartilage-based skeletons.
Blacktip sharks are easily identifiable by the black tips on their fins and are known for their jumping behavior.
Zebra sharks are named for their juvenile striped pattern, which fades as they age.
The frilled shark has a long, eel-like body and is often called a "living fossil."
Catsharks are small, bottom-dwelling sharks that are often recognized by their cat-like eyes.
Goblin sharks have a long, flat snout and retractable jaws, giving them a bizarre appearance.
Dogfish sharks are small to medium-sized and are often studied due to their adaptability.
The chimaera, or ghost shark, is a relative of sharks but belongs to a different class called Holocephali.
Tiger sharks have serrated teeth, ideal for cutting through tough prey like sea turtles.
Bull sharks are territorial and aggressive, living in both freshwater and saltwater.
Sharks can detect electric fields emitted by other creatures using organs called ampullae of Lorenzini.
